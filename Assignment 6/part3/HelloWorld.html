The building blocks of a language

Phonology
is the branch of linguistics that studies phonemes, what phonemes there are, and how they form words when combined. Phonemes are the most basic units of a language, that may or may not be meaningful, and form the sound system of language.
Morphology
is the branch of linguistics that deals with morphemes and their meanings. A morpheme is the smallest meaningful lexical item in a language.
Syntax
is the method of combining words in a senseful manner to form sentences. It is the "infinite use of finite media".

Some important facts

  • It has been found that children start learning language very early on in life, and can differentiate between all the phonemes of natural language. They are even sensitive to word order by the age of 1.
  • At around 1.5 years old, language-learning speeds up by quite a lot, and kids start forming two-word "telegraphic" sentences. They also develop function morphemes (like the plural -s, and the past tense -ed).
  • It has been found that if one begins learning a language after the age of 10, they are much less likely to attain fluency in it.
  • Animals may have communication methods, but they don't contain any phonology, morphology, or syntax. So, they don't really have a "Language".
